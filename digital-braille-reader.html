PROJECT OVERVIEW

Enhancing accessibility through tactile technology

This device enhances information accessibility for visually impaired individuals by converting digital text into tactile braille patterns. The portable, battery-powered device utilizes six vibration generators to encode and communicate alphabet letters through finger-based tactile sensation. The system incorporates Bluetooth connectivity for seamless file transfer from a smartphone and includes onboard memory for PDF storage.

Employing a sense-plan-act paradigm with full autonomy, the device operates through explicit user actions, mapping input to the braille alphabet across high, mid, and low-level controls. This project addresses the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities' mandate for equal access to information and assistive technologies.

While existing electronic braille displays are available, they are often prohibitively expensive ($700-$4000) and bulky (keyboard-sized with 20-40 cell refreshable displays). Our proposed device offers a more compact, affordable alternative—mimicking a smartphone form factor—potentially improving quality of life and autonomy for visually impaired users in an increasingly digital world.

BACKGROUND & MOTIVATION

Bridging the accessibility gap in digital reading

In today's increasingly digital world, access to information is crucial for participation in society, education, and personal development. However, for individuals with visual impairments, this access can be significantly challenging. The United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (2006) emphasized equal access to information for disabled people, including the use of assistive technologies to enhance communication and information accessibility.

The Problem with Current Solutions

Braille, a tactile reading and writing system developed by Louis Braille in 1824, has long been a vital tool for visually impaired individuals to access written information. While electronic braille displays exist in the market, they often come with significant limitations:

  • Cost Barrier: Current devices range from $700 to $4000, making them inaccessible to many users
  • Size and Portability: Typically keyboard-sized with 20-40 cell refreshable displays, limiting everyday use
  • Limited Functionality: Many devices lack seamless integration with modern digital platforms
  • Complexity: Often require extensive training and setup for effective use

Our Vision

To address these challenges, we developed a novel, portable digital braille reader that bridges the gap between traditional braille and modern digital text. Our device is designed to be compact (smartphone-sized), battery-powered, affordable, and easily integrated with existing digital workflows. By leveraging vibrotactile feedback and wireless connectivity, we created an accessible solution that empowers visually impaired individuals to consume digital content independently.

DESIGN CHALLENGE

Creating an intuitive, portable braille interface

The primary challenge was developing a device that could translate digital text into tactile braille patterns in real-time while remaining compact, affordable, and energy-efficient. Unlike traditional refreshable braille displays that use mechanical pins, we needed to create a vibrotactile system that users could understand intuitively through touch alone.

Technical Challenges

  • Vibration Isolation: Preventing vibration propagation through the casing that would make it difficult to distinguish which buzzer was active
  • Braille Encoding: Mapping 7-bit ASCII characters to 6-bit braille patterns efficiently for real-time translation
  • Bluetooth Reliability: Maintaining stable BLE connection during high-throughput data transfer without disconnections
  • Power Management: Balancing continuous Bluetooth advertising with battery life constraints
  • User Experience: Designing an interface that blind users could navigate without visual cues
  • Ergonomics: Creating a compact form factor that fits comfortably in one hand for extended reading sessions

The solution required integrating mechanical design (3D-printed casing), firmware development (ESP32-S3 microcontroller), wireless communication (BLE protocol), and mobile app development (Flutter framework)—all working together to create a seamless assistive technology experience.

HARDWARE DESIGN

3D-printed ergonomic enclosure

The physical encasing for the braille reader was designed using SolidWorks and fabricated using PrusaSlicer Version 2.8.1. The casing was engineered to be easy to hold, small enough to carry around, and comfortable to use with one hand for extended periods.

Design Considerations

  • Ergonomic Form Factor: Support structure and rounded edges designed to provide secure grip and comfort during long operations
  • Vibration Isolation: Casing material carefully selected to dampen vibration propagation, allowing users to distinguish each buzzer individually
  • Component Layout: Strategic placement of six buzzers corresponding to the six dots of standard braille cells
  • Durability: Structural integrity verified through multiple drop tests, withstanding high drops without cracks or visible damage
  • Portability: Compact smartphone-like form factor enabling discrete, everyday carry

Vibrational Mechanics

One critical design element was ensuring that users could distinguish which buzzer was creating a vibration, especially when multiple buzzers were active simultaneously. The casing was engineered to dampen incoming signals appropriately, preventing cross-talk between buzzers while maintaining sufficient tactile feedback intensity for reliable character recognition.

The support structure incorporated internal baffles and material selection optimized for vibration isolation, ensuring each of the six buzzers could be independently perceived by the user's fingertips.

FIRMWARE ARCHITECTURE

High-speed embedded processing on ESP32-S3

The firmware was meticulously designed for high-speed processing, energy efficiency, and asynchronous handling of tactile output. The microcontroller used was a TinyS3 (ESP32-S3 Board), chosen for its robust BLE capabilities, low power consumption, and sufficient processing power for real-time braille encoding.

Figure 2: TinyS3 (ESP32-S3 Board) microcontroller used for firmware processing and BLE communication

Braille Encoding System

Upon receiving data encoded as 7-bit ASCII values, the firmware utilizes a highly optimized lookup table stored in flash memory to transform these into 6-bit braille representations suitable for driving the device's array of buzzers. This transformation is performed with minimal computational overhead, leveraging precomputed mappings to ensure real-time responsiveness.

The 6-bit braille pattern directly maps to the six buzzers, where each bit position corresponds to one of the standard braille dot positions. For example, the letter 'A' in braille (dot 1) activates only the first buzzer, while 'W' (dots 2-4-5-6) activates four buzzers simultaneously.

Asynchronous Message Queue

To manage output efficiently, the firmware implements a non-blocking, asynchronous queuing system. Incoming messages are enqueued and dispatched sequentially to the buzzers, allowing for parallel processing of input and output tasks. This asynchronous design prevents system overload by ensuring that newly received messages do not interrupt ongoing operations, thus maintaining consistent and reliable tactile feedback.

The queue architecture enables smooth reading experiences even when receiving long text streams, buffering incoming characters and serving them at an appropriate rate for user comprehension.

Bluetooth Low Energy Implementation

BLE communication is integral to the firmware's functionality. The device operates as a BLE peripheral, dynamically advertising itself on available channels (RF37, RF38, RF39). Upon establishing a connection, the firmware employs a connection-handling protocol, ensuring a stable link with the host device. The connection is maintained even under conditions of high data throughput, preventing unintended disconnection during read/write operations.

To further enhance energy efficiency, the ESP32-S3 enters a low-power state during idle periods, and BLE advertising intervals are optimized to conserve battery power. Real-time responsiveness is achieved through precise timing controls, ensuring tactile feedback is delivered at an appropriate frequency for user comprehension.

Error Handling

The firmware gracefully handles invalid inputs, ignoring unsupported characters without interrupting the tactile output. This ensures a smooth reading experience even when encountering non-standard characters or formatting symbols in digital documents.

MOBILE APPLICATION

Flutter-based companion app for iOS and Android

The application was designed with a focus on usability, reliability, and intuitive interaction. Leveraging the Flutter framework, we implemented the Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) communication protocol to optimize performance. This approach minimized system load by awakening devices only when necessary, reducing the number of active radio frequency channels to enhance connection speed, and transmitting minimal data packets to ensure efficiency across all systems.

BLE Communication Flow

The app uses custom-built software to facilitate BLE scanning and device discovery for Android and iOS platforms. This involves leveraging platform-specific Bluetooth APIs while providing a unified interface for users. For connection, we start the scan with custom filters so that unwanted Bluetooth devices do not populate the user interface.

The phone scans the primary advertising channels (RF37, RF38, RF39) and listens for any advertising device that fits the user's filtering preferences. Once connected over BLE, we ensure not to send any unnecessary information to save bandwidth and power on the device. The user is then sent to another screen where they can facilitate the connection and serve the braille reader with encoded information.

The information is sent as a 7-bit ASCII value over the available advertising channel and encoded on the device into 6-bit braille characters for activation on the buzzers.

SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE

Complete end-to-end information flow

Sense-Plan-Act Paradigm

The device operates through a classical robotics control paradigm adapted for assistive technology:

  • Sense: User initiates text input via smartphone app (typing, PDF upload, or file selection)
  • Plan: App processes text, converts to 7-bit ASCII, and streams over BLE to device
  • Act: Firmware decodes to 6-bit braille patterns and activates corresponding buzzers for tactile output

Data Pipeline

The complete information flow from digital text to tactile output:

  • Input Layer: User selects text source (manual entry, PDF, or clipboard) via Flutter app
  • Transmission Layer: Text encoded as 7-bit ASCII values and transmitted via BLE advertising channel
  • Processing Layer: ESP32-S3 firmware performs lookup table conversion from 7-bit to 6-bit braille
  • Queue Management: Asynchronous message queue buffers characters for sequential output
  • Output Layer: Six buzzers activated according to braille pattern, providing tactile feedback to user's fingers

Component Integration

  • ESP32-S3 Microcontroller: Central processing unit managing BLE communication and buzzer control
  • Six Vibration Motors: Buzzers positioned to correspond with standard 6-dot braille cell layout
  • Battery Management: Rechargeable battery with power optimization for extended operation
  • 3D-Printed Enclosure: Ergonomic casing providing structural support and vibration isolation
  • Flutter Mobile App: Cross-platform application for text input and device control

TECHNICAL IMPLEMENTATION

Building the complete assistive system

1. Braille Encoding Algorithm

The core of the system is the efficient mapping between 7-bit ASCII characters and 6-bit braille patterns. The firmware maintains a precomputed lookup table in flash memory that directly translates incoming characters to buzzer activation patterns. This eliminates the need for real-time computation, ensuring instantaneous response when characters are received.

For example:

  • Letter 'A' (ASCII 65) → Braille pattern 100000 → Buzzer 1 activates
  • Letter 'B' (ASCII 66) → Braille pattern 110000 → Buzzers 1 and 2 activate
  • Letter 'W' (ASCII 87) → Braille pattern 011110 → Buzzers 2, 3, 4, and 5 activate

2. BLE Connection Management

The device continuously advertises on primary BLE channels, making itself discoverable to the companion app. Once a connection is established, the firmware transitions from advertising mode to connected mode, dedicating resources to maintaining the link and processing incoming data.

Connection parameters are optimized for:

  • Minimal latency for real-time text streaming
  • Sufficient bandwidth for continuous character transmission
  • Low power consumption through adaptive advertising intervals
  • Robust reconnection protocols for seamless recovery from temporary disconnections

3. Asynchronous Output Control

The firmware implements a non-blocking queue system where incoming 7-bit values are converted to 6-bit patterns and placed in a FIFO (First-In-First-Out) buffer. A separate output task sequentially processes the queue, activating buzzers according to each braille pattern and maintaining appropriate timing between characters for user comprehension.

This architecture allows the system to continue receiving new characters while still outputting previous ones, preventing message loss and maintaining smooth reading flow even during burst transmissions.

LIMITATIONS & FUTURE WORK

Known constraints and improvement opportunities

1. Bandwidth Limitations

Although BLE is reliable and fast, it has bandwidth limitations that may be difficult to overcome depending on what type of information is intended to be streamed. For example, large PDFs would need to be encoded and sent piecewise over the system in order to not overload the communication channel. Systems like these would require significant work and testing, as the more information sent, the higher the likelihood of a transmission failure.

2. Vibration Dampening Challenges

One of the main complaints received from users was difficulty in discerning which buzzer created a vibration, especially in scenarios where multiple buzzers were active simultaneously. This issue arose because vibrations generated by the buzzers tended to propagate through the casing material, making it challenging for users to pinpoint which specific buzzer was pulsing.

As a result, users implicitly relied on the onboard LEDs to identify active buzzers, as the light cues provided clear indication of which buzzer was vibrating at any given time. This issue created a reliance on visual cues in order to use the system, which is not an accessible solution for blind people.

3. Mobile App Accessibility

Both Android and iOS devices offer specialized accessibility options designed to meet the needs of blind and visually impaired users, including screen readers and voice control. In our app, we began integrating these accessibility features to ensure our platform is usable by blind or visually impaired users. This includes incorporating code changes to support screen readers, enabling the app to read aloud text content and provide verbal descriptions of interactive elements.

However, we identified several areas requiring further attention to improve user experience. Specifically, some buttons and interactive elements do not get correctly presented or identified in screen reader mode. As a result, navigating through even the most basic functions of the app becomes challenging for users relying on auditory cues alone.

Proposed Improvements

  • Enhanced Vibration Isolation: Redesign internal casing structure with improved baffles and damping materials to better isolate each buzzer's vibration
  • Alternative Actuation Methods: Explore piezoelectric actuators or solenoids for more discrete, localized tactile feedback
  • Improved Data Compression: Implement efficient encoding schemes for transmitting large documents over BLE without exceeding bandwidth limits
  • Full Screen Reader Support: Complete accessibility code refinement to ensure all interactive elements are properly labeled and navigable through auditory cues
  • Multi-line Display: Expand from single-character output to multi-character display for faster reading speeds
  • Onboard PDF Processing: Add local PDF parsing capabilities to reduce reliance on phone-side processing
